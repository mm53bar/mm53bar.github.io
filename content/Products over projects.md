The "project" model—where teams form temporarily to deliver a predefined scope—creates systemic dysfunction in software:

1. **Projects assume software is "done"**, but modern systems evolve indefinitely. Teams disband just as they grasp the context, forcing the next team to relearn everything ([[Developers spend most of their time figuring out systems]]).

2. **Build+Run teams** (owning products end-to-end) outperform project teams because:
    - They retain deep system/business knowledge ([[Stable teams compound institutional knowledge]]).
    - They prioritize long-term health (tech debt, usability) over short-term deliverables.

3. **Project incentives misalign**: Teams optimize for handoff (meeting deadlines) over sustainable outcomes. Products demand continuous ownership.

**Implications**:

- Shift funding from projects to **product streams** with ongoing teams.
- Measure outcomes (e.g., user retention, defect rates) over output (features shipped).

**Connections**:

- Contrast with [[Strategic opportunism]]—product teams can act opportunistically because they understand the system’s evolution.
- Supports [[Use AI to reduce toil]]: Stable teams better leverage AI’s time savings for innovation.
